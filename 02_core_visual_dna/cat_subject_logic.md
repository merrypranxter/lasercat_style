# Cat Subject Logic

In `laser_cat_style`, the cat is not just a subject.
The cat is the operating system.

This file defines what kinds of cats work in the style, how cats function visually, and what makes a cat image suitable for the aesthetic.

---

# Core principle

The cat must be more than present.
It must be:
- central
- readable
- funny
- iconic
- compositionally load-bearing

A random cat pasted into a scene is not enough.
The cat needs to drive the image's joke, energy, or authority.

---

# What kinds of cats work best

## 1. Cats with strong expressions

The best cats often look:
- startled
- vacant
- smug
- furious
- confused
- battle-ready
- accidentally majestic
- chemically compromised
- deeply stupid in a lovable way

Facial expression carries a lot of the humor.

Great examples include:
- wide eyes
- tiny mouth open in horror
- goblin scream
- blank stare
- weirdly judgmental look
- over-serious face in stupid context

## 2. Cats with awkward body language

Strong poses often include:
- stiff upright stance
- sprawled flop
- loaf posture
- mid-jump weirdness
- paws extended in an absurd way
- flailing limb shapes
- frozen action pose
- tiny standing cat silhouette
- weirdly vertical cat body

The style likes bodily awkwardness because it increases the cutout absurdity.

## 3. Cats with clear silhouettes

Even when the cutout is bad, the cat should usually remain readable from a distance.

Good subject candidates often have:
- distinct ears
- strong head outline
- visible paws
- recognizable loaf or long-body form
- readable facial zone

If the cat is visually muddy before collage work begins, the scene can collapse fast.

## 4. Cats that tolerate visual abuse

Some cat photos survive:
- cutout roughness
- glow effects
- resizing
- scale exaggeration
- low-res transformation
- object layering

Those cats are ideal.
Highly delicate or subtle cat images often fail under `laser_cat_style` treatment.

---

# Types of cat images that perform well

## Hero cat
A single central cat dominating the composition.

## Floating cat
A cat isolated against a cosmic or void background.

## Action cat
A jumping, falling, swatting, lunging, or mid-motion cat.

## Face cat
A close-up cat face used for lasers, rainbow puke, drug-cat escalation, or giant icon mode.

## Object-cat hybrid
A cat merged with food, props, or costume elements.

## Giant cat
A cat scaled impossibly large for kaiju/god mode.

## Tiny witness cat
A smaller cat used as an absurd observer or secondary icon.

---

# Desirable subject qualities

## Readability
The cat remains clearly legible after collage treatment.

## Expressive stupidity
The cat appears to be doing too much, too little, or the wrong thing.

## Elastic scale behavior
The cat still works when shrunk or enlarged absurdly.

## Cutout resilience
The subject survives hard masking, rough edges, or low-res compositing.

## Icon potential
The cat can become emblematic, not merely photographic.

---

# Subject roles

A cat can function in several ways inside the style.

## 1. Cat as deity
The cat appears all-powerful, imposing, or cosmic.

## 2. Cat as idiot
The cat looks gloriously unqualified for the scene.

## 3. Cat as destroyer
The cat causes laser damage, scale havoc, or rainbow violence.

## 4. Cat as passenger
The cat rides pizza, shark, taco, unicorn, sandwich, etc.

## 5. Cat as throne-occupant
The cat sits on the absurd object as if entitled to it.

## 6. Cat as emission-source
The eyes or mouth become the action center.

## 7. Cat as witness
The cat simply exists in the chaos, enhancing absurdity through nonchalance.

## 8. Cat as decorative icon
The cat works almost like a sticker emblem or symbolic insert.

These roles are expanded further in `cat_power_roles.md`.

---

# What kinds of cats are less useful

Not every cat image works.

Weak candidates often include:

## Overly polished studio cats
Too clean, too perfect, too commercial.

## Low-expression cats with muddy visibility
No readable face, no clear silhouette, no compositional presence.

## Overcomplicated fur detail that collapses under cutout
Very fine edge detail can become ugly in the wrong way.

## Hyper-cute posed cats
These can drift toward kawaii merch aesthetics unless aggressively corrupted.

## Overly realistic dramatic cat portraits
These tend to become fantasy art or poster art instead of meme collage.

# Breed and appearance flexibility

The style welcomes all kinds of cats:
- fluffy cats
- shorthair cats
- tabbies
- black cats
- white cats
- orange idiots
- Siamese-like cats
- chunky cats
- skinny goblins
- kitten chaos
- elderly weirdos

What matters is not breed purity.
What matters is:
- expression
- pose
- silhouette
- collage usefulness
- absurd potential

Some cats naturally read better in certain branches:
- fluffy white cats can dominate psychedelic and rainbow branches
- black cats work well in graphic silhouette and beam-god modes
- orange cats are excellent for idiot energy and food scenes
- gray cats often work well in glam or cosmic collage modes

But none of this is mandatory.

---

# Subject transformation tolerance

A good `laser_cat_style` cat can tolerate one or more of the following transformations:
- resized 5x too large
- resized 10x too small
- rough cutout edge
- halo glow
- saturated recoloring
- rainbow emission insertion
- accessory placement
- object hybridization
- background mismatch
- partial compression damage

That tolerance is essential.
The style often improves when the cat survives a little abuse.

---

# Cat centrality test

A simple test:

## Passes strongly if:
- the image still feels "about the cat" after adding chaos

## Weakens if:
- the cat becomes just another floating object

## Fails if:
- the cat could be replaced by any random animal or object without changing the image's identity

The cat must matter specifically as a cat.

---

# Bottom line

In `laser_cat_style`, the ideal cat is:
- expressive
- readable
- collage-resilient
- absurdity-compatible
- strong at both majesty and dumbassery

The cat is not decorative filler.
It is the engine, the icon, the victim, the god, and the punchline.
